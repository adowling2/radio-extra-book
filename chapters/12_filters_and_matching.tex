\chapter{Filters, Matching Networks, and Transformers}
\label{ch:filters}
\section{Why Filters Matter in Amateur Radio}
Filters select desired signals and reject unwanted ones. They appear in receiver
front ends, IF stages, audio stages, transmitter outputs, anti-aliasing paths,
power supplies, and interference cures. A filter is nothing more than a circuit
whose transfer function has been shaped on purpose---poles placed to form the
passband, zeros placed to notch the stopband---so everything from Part~II applies
directly.

\section{Basic Filter Types}
\begin{description}
\item[Low-pass] Passes low frequencies and attenuates high frequencies.
\item[High-pass] Passes high frequencies and attenuates low frequencies.
\item[Band-pass] Passes a frequency band.
\item[Band-stop or notch] Rejects a frequency band.
\end{description}

\section{A Filter from First Principles}
Rather than take filter formulas on faith, let us build one the way a
first-principles modeler would---from the physics of the circuit, up through the
four views of \cref{ch:linsys}: physical network \(\to\) differential equation
\(\to\) state-space \(\to\) transfer function \(\to\) frequency response.

\subsection{First Order: the RC Low-Pass}
The RC low-pass (\cref{fig:rc-lp}) is the first-order system of \cref{ch:rc} doing
a filtering job. KVL and the capacitor law give the model in one line each:
\[
v_{\mathrm{in}}=R\,i+v_o,\quad i=C\frac{\dd v_o}{\dd t}
\;\Rightarrow\;
\underbrace{RC\frac{\dd v_o}{\dd t}+v_o=v_{\mathrm{in}}}_{\text{ODE}}
\;\Rightarrow\;
\underbrace{\dot{x}=-\tfrac{1}{RC}x+\tfrac{1}{RC}v_{\mathrm{in}}}_{\text{state-space, }x=v_o}
\;\Rightarrow\;
\underbrace{G(s)=\frac{1}{1+RCs}}_{\text{transfer function}} .
\]
One energy store gives one pole, at \(s=-1/RC\); the frequency response is flat to
the corner \(\omega_c=1/RC\) and then falls at \(-\SI{20}{\decibel}\) per decade.

\subsection{Second Order: the LC Low-Pass}
Add an inductor and you add an energy store---hence a second pole and the
possibility of resonance. The series \(R\)--\(L\)--\(C\) low-pass
(\cref{fig:lc-section}), which is the RLC circuit of \cref{ch:rlc} used as a
filter, develops the same way. KVL around the loop and the capacitor law for the
output node give
\[
v_{\mathrm{in}}=R\,i+L\frac{\dd i}{\dd t}+v_o,
\qquad
i=C\frac{\dd v_o}{\dd t},
\]
and eliminating \(i\) yields the second-order ODE
\[
LC\frac{\dd^2 v_o}{\dd t^2}+RC\frac{\dd v_o}{\dd t}+v_o=v_{\mathrm{in}}.
\]
With states \(x_1=v_o\;(=v_C)\) and \(x_2=i\;(=i_L)\)---one per energy store---the
state-space model is
\[
\dot{x}_1=\frac{1}{C}x_2,
\qquad
\dot{x}_2=-\frac{1}{L}x_1-\frac{R}{L}x_2+\frac{1}{L}v_{\mathrm{in}},
\]
and the Laplace transform gives the transfer function
\[
G(s)=\frac{1}{LC\,s^2+RC\,s+1}
=\frac{\wzero^2}{s^2+\dfrac{\wzero}{Q}\,s+\wzero^2},
\qquad
\wzero=\frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}},
\qquad
Q=\frac{1}{R}\sqrt{\frac{L}{C}} .
\]
This is exactly the standard second-order form of \cref{ch:linsys}, so its
frequency response is a low-pass that falls at \(-\SI{40}{\decibel}\) per decade
(two poles) with a resonant peak of height \(\sim Q\) near \(\wzero\) when
\(Q>1/\sqrt2\), as in \cref{ch:splane}.

\begin{controlsbox}
\textbf{The two design knobs fall right out.} From the first-principles model,
\(\wzero=1/\sqrt{LC}\) sets the cutoff frequency and \(Q=(1/R)\sqrt{L/C}\) sets
the peaking (how close the pole pair sits to the \(\jj\omega\) axis). Scaling
\(L\) and \(C\) together slides the whole response in frequency; changing the
resistance (loss, or the source/load termination) changes only the damping. Every
filter design is some version of choosing these two, times the number of poles.
\end{controlsbox}

\section{Order, Energy Stores, and Roll-Off}
The pattern generalizes. Each independent energy-storage element (an \(L\) or a
\(C\)) contributes one pole, so an \(n\)th-order filter has \(n\) reactive
elements and rolls off at about \(-20n\,\si{\decibel}\) per decade far above
cutoff (each pole adding \(-\SI{20}{\decibel}\)/decade, each zero
\(+\SI{20}{\decibel}\)/decade). Steeper skirts require more poles, hence more
inductors and capacitors. The \emph{order} is thus both a circuit fact (how many
reactive parts) and a response fact (how fast it rolls off).

\section{Designing and Tuning a Filter}
Turn the derivation around and you have the design procedure. A filter is
specified by a target frequency response; designing it means \emph{placing the
poles and zeros} to meet that target and then realizing them with a circuit.
There are three independent choices:
\begin{description}
\item[Cutoff frequency] scale all \(L\) and \(C\) together: \(\wzero=1/\sqrt{LC}\)
shifts the whole response along the frequency axis without changing its shape.
\item[Order] the number of poles (reactive elements) sets skirt steepness,
\(-20n\,\si{\decibel}\) per decade.
\item[Shape (the ``family'')] for a given order, \emph{where} the poles sit trades
passband flatness against transition sharpness against phase linearity.
\end{description}

That last choice is what the named families are: different rules for placing the
poles (and zeros) of a given order. \Cref{fig:filter-families} shows them at the
same order, and \cref{fig:butterworth-poles} shows the archetype---Butterworth
poles equally spaced on a circle.

\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{}L{0.19\textwidth}Y@{}}
\toprule
Family & Pole/zero placement and consequence \\
\midrule
Butterworth & Poles equally spaced on a semicircle of radius \(\wzero\) in the left half-plane. Maximally flat passband, no ripple, moderate transition. \\
Chebyshev & Poles on an ellipse pushed closer to the \(\jj\omega\) axis. Sharper cutoff at the price of passband ripple and worse phase/transient behavior. \\
Elliptic (Cauer; ``elliptical'' in the pool) & Chebyshev-like poles plus transmission \emph{zeros} on the \(\jj\omega\) axis in the stopband. Sharpest transition; ripple in both bands. \\
Bessel & Poles positioned for maximally flat \emph{group delay} (linear phase); gentle amplitude cutoff. Good for pulse fidelity. \\
Crystal / cavity & Very high-\(Q\) resonators (poles hugging the axis) for narrow, highly selective band-pass shapes. \\
DSP FIR & No feedback, so no poles away from the origin (all zeros); can be exactly linear phase. \\
DSP IIR & Recursive: has poles, so it mimics analog families efficiently but with nonlinear phase. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}

The \emph{transition band} is the frequency span between the passband edge and the
stopband; a ``moderate'' transition (Butterworth) is wider than the ``sharp''
transition of a Chebyshev or elliptic filter of the same order.
\Cref{fig:filter-families} makes the flatness-versus-sharpness-versus-ripple trade
concrete.

Finally, the same target poles can be \emph{realized} several ways: a passive LC
ladder, an active Sallen--Key stage (\cref{ch:active}) that makes complex poles
without inductors, a crystal or cavity when very high \(Q\) is needed, or a DSP
filter (FIR/IIR) that places the poles and zeros in software. Same poles,
different hardware---so ``customizing'' a filter means choosing the order (skirt
steepness), the family (the flatness/sharpness/phase trade), the cutoff (by
scaling \(L\) and \(C\)), and the realization that suits the frequency range and
available parts.

\section{Impedance Matching}
An impedance-matching network does two jobs:
\begin{enumerate}
\item cancel the reactive part of a complex load, and
\item transform the remaining resistance to the desired system impedance.
\end{enumerate}
In a \(\SI{50}{\ohm}\) RF system, matching maximizes power transfer and keeps
transmission-line reflections controlled.

\subsection{The L-Network}
The simplest matching network uses two reactances. To match two real resistances
\(R_{\text{hi}}>R_{\text{lo}}\), place a series reactance on the low-resistance
side and a shunt reactance on the high-resistance side. The network operating
\(Q\) is set by the resistance ratio:
\[
Q=\sqrt{\frac{R_{\text{hi}}}{R_{\text{lo}}}-1},
\qquad
X_{\text{series}}=Q\,R_{\text{lo}},
\qquad
X_{\text{shunt}}=\frac{R_{\text{hi}}}{Q}.
\]
The two reactances are of opposite type (one inductor, one capacitor); choosing
which is the inductor sets whether the network is low-pass or high-pass
(\cref{fig:lnetwork}). An L-network has no free parameter left over, so its
bandwidth is fixed once the resistances are chosen.

\subsection{Pi and T Networks}
Adding a third element gives a \(\pi\) or T network, which can be viewed as two
L-networks back to back that meet at an internal \emph{virtual resistance}
\(R_v\). Choosing \(R_v\) below both terminations sets a loaded \(Q\) of roughly
\[
Q_{\text{loaded}}\approx\sqrt{\frac{R_{\max}}{R_v}-1},
\]
so \(R_v\) is the extra degree of freedom that lets the designer pick the loaded
\(Q\)---and therefore the bandwidth and harmonic rejection---independently of the
resistance ratio. This is why transmitter output and antenna-tuner networks are
usually \(\pi\) or T: they match \emph{and} set selectivity. Higher loaded \(Q\)
narrows bandwidth and improves harmonic suppression at the cost of higher
circulating currents and losses.

\begin{mistakebox}
Matching is not the same thing as filtering. A network can do both, but the
concepts are distinct: matching controls the impedance seen by a source or line,
while filtering controls frequency-dependent transmission. A \(\pi\)-network tuner
happens to be a low-pass filter too, which is a feature, not the definition.
\end{mistakebox}

\section{Transformers}
For an ideal transformer,
\[
\frac{V_s}{V_p}=\frac{N_s}{N_p},
\qquad
\frac{I_s}{I_p}=\frac{N_p}{N_s},
\qquad
Z_p=\left(\frac{N_p}{N_s}\right)^2 Z_s.
\]
Impedance transforms as the turns ratio \emph{squared}. \textbf{Core saturation}
should be avoided: every magnetic core has a maximum flux density it can carry,
and once the drive pushes it near that limit the core's permeability collapses, so
the inductance drops and the winding current shoots up. The result is distortion,
heating, and loss of the intended impedance transformation---the reason
transformers and inductors are rated for a maximum voltage-time product (or power)
at a given frequency.

\section{Worked Example: Quarter-Wave and L-Network}
To match a real \(\SI{200}{\ohm}\) load to a \(\SI{50}{\ohm}\) system with a
lossless quarter-wave transformer,
\[
Z_t=\sqrt{Z_0Z_L}=\sqrt{(50)(200)}=\SI{100}{\ohm}.
\]
The same match with an L-network gives
\[
Q=\sqrt{\frac{200}{50}-1}=\sqrt{3}\approx1.73,
\qquad
X_{\text{series}}=1.73\times50\approx\SI{87}{\ohm},
\qquad
X_{\text{shunt}}=\frac{200}{1.73}\approx\SI{116}{\ohm}.
\]
Both realize the match; the quarter-wave version needs a line one quarter
wavelength long at the design frequency, while the L-network is lumped and
narrower band.
