\chapter{First- and Second-Order Systems: Time and State Space}
\label{ch:linsys}
Before any specific circuit, this chapter reviews the linear-systems machinery
that the rest of the book uses, in the \emph{time and state-space} view:
differential equations, state-space models, step responses, damping, and
feedback. Its companion, \cref{ch:splane}, reads the same systems in the
\emph{frequency and s-plane} view (Bode plots, poles, zeros, root locus).
Everything here is generic---the same mathematics whether the system is an RC
filter, a mechanical suspension, or a chemical reactor---and Part~III then shows
that each amateur-radio circuit is one of these systems in electrical clothing.

\section{Four Equivalent Views of a Linear System}
A linear time-invariant system can be written four equivalent ways, and moving
between them is the central skill of this book:
\begin{enumerate}
\item a \textbf{differential equation} relating output \(y(t)\) to input \(u(t)\);
\item a \textbf{state-space model} \(\dot{\state}=A\state+Bu,\; y=C\state+Du\);
\item a \textbf{transfer function} \(G(s)=Y(s)/U(s)\) in the Laplace variable
\(s\);
\item a \textbf{frequency response} \(G(\jj\omega)\), the transfer function on the
imaginary axis.
\end{enumerate}
This chapter builds the first three and the time response; \cref{ch:splane} takes
up the fourth.

\begin{controlsbox}
\textbf{Refresher: how the four views connect.} Taking the Laplace transform of
the state-space model (zero initial conditions) and eliminating \(\state\) gives
\[
G(s)=C\,(sI-A)^{-1}B+D .
\]
The \emph{poles} of \(G\) are the eigenvalues of \(A\); they are the system's
natural modes. Setting \(s=\jj\omega\) samples the response to a steady sinusoid
of frequency \(\omega\). So the eigenvalues you compute for stability, the poles
you read on an s-plane, and the peaks you see on a Bode plot are all the same
numbers viewed differently.
\end{controlsbox}

\section{Laplace Transforms and Block Diagrams}
The explicit bridge from a differential equation to a transfer function is the
Laplace transform, and three facts carry the whole load. With zero initial
conditions, differentiation becomes multiplication by \(s\) and integration
becomes division:
\[
\mathcal{L}\{\dot f\}=sF(s),\qquad
\mathcal{L}\{\ddot f\}=s^2F(s),\qquad
\mathcal{L}\Bigl\{\textstyle\int_0^t f\,\dd t'\Bigr\}=\frac{F(s)}{s}.
\]

\begin{controlsbox}
\textbf{Refresher: Laplace generalizes phasors.} The phasor substitution
\(\dd/\dd t\to\jj\omega\) is the Laplace rule \(\dd/\dd t\to s\) evaluated on the
imaginary axis \(s=\jj\omega\). The Laplace variable \(s=\sigma+\jj\omega\) simply
adds the growth/decay coordinate \(\sigma\), which is what lets it describe
transients and stability, not just steady sinusoids.
\end{controlsbox}

Applying the derivative rule to a linear ODE turns calculus into algebra. The two
systems of this chapter transform directly from equation to transfer function:
\[
\tau\dot y+y=Ku
\;\xrightarrow{\;\mathcal L\;}\;
(\tau s+1)Y=KU
\;\Rightarrow\;
G(s)=\frac{K}{\tau s+1},
\]
\[
\ddot y+2\zeta\omega_0\dot y+\omega_0^2 y=\omega_0^2u
\;\xrightarrow{\;\mathcal L\;}\;
(s^2+2\zeta\omega_0 s+\omega_0^2)Y=\omega_0^2U
\;\Rightarrow\;
G(s)=\frac{\omega_0^2}{s^2+2\zeta\omega_0 s+\omega_0^2}.
\]
This ODE-to-transfer-function route is exactly what the modeling chapters of
Part~III use once they have written a circuit's differential equation from KVL
and KCL.

A transfer function is drawn as a block with an input and an output arrow, and
larger systems are built by wiring blocks together. Three rules cover almost
everything (\cref{fig:blocks}): cascaded blocks \emph{multiply}
(\(G=G_1G_2\)); parallel blocks \emph{add} (\(G=G_1+G_2\)); and a negative
feedback loop collapses to
\[
G_{\mathrm{cl}}=\frac{G}{1+GH}.
\]

\section{First-Order Systems}
The canonical first-order system has one state and one time constant \(\tau\):
\[
\tau\,\dot{y}+y=K\,u
\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad
\dot{x}=-\frac{1}{\tau}x+\frac{K}{\tau}u,\quad y=x .
\]
Its single pole is real and negative,
\[
G(s)=\frac{K}{\tau s+1},
\qquad
s_{\text{pole}}=-\frac{1}{\tau}.
\]

\subsection{The Step Response}
For a unit step input the output rises exponentially to its final value,
\[
y(t)=K\bigl(1-\ee^{-t/\tau}\bigr).
\]
The time constant \(\tau\) is the natural clock of the system:
after one \(\tau\) the response has covered \(1-\ee^{-1}\approx 63.2\%\) of the
way, and after about \(5\tau\) it is settled for practical purposes
(\cref{fig:step1}).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{step_first_order}
\caption{First-order step response. One time constant reaches \(63.2\%\); five
time constants are effectively settled.}
\label{fig:step1}
\end{figure}

\begin{physicalbox}
A first-order system has a single real pole at \(-1/\tau\), and no imaginary
part, so it can never overshoot or ring. Its distance from the origin, \(1/\tau\),
is the decay rate in time and (as \cref{ch:splane} shows) the corner frequency in
the frequency domain---one number, two readings.
\end{physicalbox}

\subsection{Worked Example}
A first-order system has \(K=5\) and \(\tau=\SI{2}{\milli\second}\). Its pole is
at \(-1/\tau=-\SI{500}{\per\second}\); the step response reaches \(63.2\%\) of
\(5\) (that is, \(3.16\)) after \SI{2}{\milli\second} and is settled after about
\SI{10}{\milli\second}.

\section{Second-Order Systems}
Two independent energy stores give a second-order system, written in
\emph{standard form} as
\[
\ddot{y}+2\zeta\omega_0\,\dot{y}+\omega_0^2\,y=\omega_0^2\,u .
\]
The two parameters are the \textbf{undamped natural frequency} \(\omega_0\) and
the dimensionless \textbf{damping ratio} \(\zeta\). A companion-form state-space
model uses \(x_1=y\), \(x_2=\dot{y}\):
\[
\dot{\state}=
\mat{0 & 1\\ -\omega_0^2 & -2\zeta\omega_0}\state
+\mat{0\\ \omega_0^2}u,
\qquad
y=\mat{1 & 0}\state .
\]
The characteristic polynomial and poles are
\[
s^2+2\zeta\omega_0 s+\omega_0^2=0,
\qquad
s=-\zeta\omega_0\pm\jj\,\omega_0\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}
=-\zeta\omega_0\pm\jj\,\omega_d,
\]
where \(\omega_d=\omega_0\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}\) is the \textbf{damped natural
frequency}. \Cref{ch:splane} plots where these poles sit and what that does to
the frequency response; here we read them in the time domain.

\begin{controlsbox}
\textbf{Refresher: \(\zeta\), \(Q\), and the damping regimes.} The damping ratio
\(\zeta\) sorts second-order systems into three behaviors:
\begin{description}
\item[\(\zeta>1\) overdamped] two real poles; no oscillation, sluggish approach.
\item[\(\zeta=1\) critically damped] repeated real pole; fastest approach without
overshoot.
\item[\(\zeta<1\) underdamped] complex pole pair; overshoot and ringing.
\end{description}
Radio work usually states the same information as the \emph{quality factor}
\(Q=1/(2\zeta)\), so a lightly damped, high-\(Q\) resonator and an underdamped
control system are the same object.
\end{controlsbox}

\subsection{Damping You Can See}
\Cref{fig:step2} shows step responses across the regimes. A heavily damped
system (\(\zeta\ge1\)) crawls to its final value; a lightly damped one
overshoots and rings for roughly \(Q\) cycles before settling. The peak
overshoot of an underdamped step is
\[
M_p=\exp\!\left(\frac{-\pi\zeta}{\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}}\right).
\]

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{step_second_order}
\caption{Second-order step responses. Low \(\zeta\) (high \(Q\)) overshoots and
rings; \(\zeta=1\) is the fastest response without overshoot; high \(\zeta\) is
sluggish.}
\label{fig:step2}
\end{figure}

\begin{physicalbox}
\textbf{Mass--spring--damper analogy.} The standard second-order system is
exactly a mass \(m\) on a spring \(k\) with a damper \(c\):
\(m\ddot{x}+c\dot{x}+kx=F\), giving \(\omega_0=\sqrt{k/m}\) and
\(\zeta=c/(2\sqrt{km})\). Underdamped is a plucked guitar string that rings;
overdamped is a screen door with a strong closer that eases shut without
slamming; critically damped is the closer tuned to shut as fast as possible
without bouncing. The RLC circuit of Part~III is this same system with
\(L\leftrightarrow m\), \(1/C\leftrightarrow k\), and \(R\leftrightarrow c\).
\end{physicalbox}

\subsection{Worked Example}
A second-order system has \(\omega_0=2\pi(\SI{1}{\kilo\hertz})\) and
\(\zeta=0.1\), so \(Q=1/(2\zeta)=5\). The poles are at
\[
s=-\zeta\omega_0\pm\jj\omega_0\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}
\approx-628\pm\jj\,6250\ \si{\per\second}.
\]
The step overshoot is \(M_p=\exp(-\pi\cdot0.1/\sqrt{1-0.01})\approx0.73\), a
\(73\%\) overshoot---the lightly damped, ringing behavior of the top curve in
\cref{fig:step2}. \Cref{ch:splane} shows the matching \(\SI{14}{\decibel}\)
resonant peak in the frequency domain.

\section{Feedback: From Open Loop to Closed Loop}
Every system so far has been \emph{open loop}: input in, output out. Wrapping the
output back and subtracting it from the input closes the loop
(\cref{fig:blocks}b). Writing the forward-path gain as \(A(s)\) and the feedback
fraction as \(\beta\), the \textbf{closed-loop} transfer function and the
\textbf{loop gain} are
\[
A_{\mathrm{cl}}(s)=\frac{A(s)}{1+A(s)\beta},
\qquad
L(s)=A(s)\beta .
\]

The single most important consequence is the \textbf{large-loop-gain limit}: when
\(|L|\gg1\),
\[
A_{\mathrm{cl}}\to\frac{1}{\beta}.
\]
The closed-loop behavior is then set by the feedback network \(\beta\) alone,
almost independent of the forward gain \(A\). Feedback also \emph{moves the poles}.
Closing a loop around a single first-order pole \(A_0/(1+s/\omega_a)\) relocates
the pole to \(-\omega_a(1+A_0\beta)\): faster response and wider bandwidth, with
the gain reduced by the same factor---the \emph{gain--bandwidth} trade. A single
pole can never be pushed unstable by feedback, but with two or more poles the loop
can drive the poles toward, and across, the \(\jj\omega\) axis, which is how
feedback systems peak, ring, or oscillate---a motion \cref{ch:splane} makes
visible with the root locus.

\begin{controlsbox}
\textbf{Foreshadowing op-amps.} An ideal operational amplifier is a forward path
with enormous gain, \(A\to\infty\). By the large-loop-gain limit its closed-loop
gain becomes exactly \(1/\beta\), set by a handful of resistors---which is why the
inverting gain \(-R_f/R_{in}\) and non-inverting gain \(1+R_f/R_g\) of
\cref{ch:active} depend only on resistor ratios, not on the amplifier itself. The
same loop run to its stability boundary is an oscillator. Feedback is the thread
that ties together op-amps, active filters, oscillators, and voltage regulators in
Part~III.
\end{controlsbox}

\begin{exambox}
The exam never asks for \(\zeta\) or a pole plot. It asks about ringing,
settling, bandwidth, selectivity, and oscillator stability---all statements about
the poles. Learn the generic time-domain picture here, read its frequency and
s-plane face in \cref{ch:splane}, and the RC, RL, and RLC circuits of Part~III
become three readings of the same system.
\end{exambox}
