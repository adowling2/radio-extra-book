\chapter{The s-Plane: Poles, Zeros, and Natural Modes}
\label{ch:splane}
\section{One Picture Behind Every View}
The linear-systems chapter introduced poles as the eigenvalues and natural modes
of a system. This chapter makes the \emph{s-plane}---the complex plane in which
those poles live---the single diagram that ties the views together, and it adds
the other half of the picture: \emph{zeros}. Where poles say how a system
resonates and rings, zeros say which frequencies it blocks. Nothing here is a
calculation the exam asks you to perform; it is the mental model that turns the
exam's vocabulary---selectivity, bandwidth, ringing, stability, traps---into one
idea.

\begin{controlsbox}
\textbf{Refresher: poles and zeros.} Write a transfer function as a ratio of
polynomials in \(s=\sigma+\jj\omega\),
\[
G(s)=\frac{N(s)}{D(s)}.
\]
The \emph{poles} are the roots of the denominator \(D(s)\); the \emph{zeros} are
the roots of the numerator \(N(s)\). Poles are where \(G\) blows up (the system's
natural modes, equal to the eigenvalues of \(A\)); zeros are where \(G\) vanishes
(frequencies the system transmits as zero). Evaluating on the imaginary axis,
\(G(\jj\omega)\), gives the frequency response: you slide \(s\) up the
\(\jj\omega\) axis and read off gain and phase, passing near poles (peaks) and
near zeros (nulls).
\end{controlsbox}

\section{The Pole-Zero Plane}
A pole-zero plot marks poles with \(\times\) and zeros with \(\circ\), and it
captures a transfer function's behavior at a glance (\cref{fig:pz-notch}). Two
reading rules, both developed generically in \cref{ch:linsys}, carry over:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Poles set resonance and damping.} A complex pole pair sits at
\(-\alpha\pm\jj\omega_d\), a distance \(\omega_0\) from the origin at an angle
\(\theta\) from the negative real axis with \(\cos\theta=\zeta\). Distance from
the \(\jj\omega\) axis (\(\alpha=\zeta\omega_0\)) is the decay rate \emph{and} the
half-power bandwidth, and \(Q=1/(2\zeta)\). A pole hugging the axis is a high-\(Q\),
lightly damped, sharply peaked resonance.
\item \textbf{Zeros set rejection.} A zero on or near the \(\jj\omega\) axis
pulls the magnitude \emph{down} toward a null at that frequency---the mechanism of
a notch, trap, or band-stop.
\end{itemize}

\section{Stability at a Glance}
Because an unforced mode evolves as \(\ee^{s_i t}\) with
\(s_i=\sigma_i+\jj\omega_i\), the sign of the real part is the entire stability
story: left half-plane (\(\sigma_i<0\)) decays and is stable; the \(\jj\omega\)
axis (\(\sigma_i=0\)) sustains a pure oscillation---the knife-edge an oscillator
sits on; the right half-plane grows and is unstable. Zeros do not affect
stability (they are not modes), but they shape how the stable modes appear in the
response.

\begin{advancedbox}
The product of the two real poles is fixed at \(\wzero^2\) (the constant term of
the monic polynomial), so as \(R\to\infty\) one overdamped pole races toward
\(-\infty\) while the other creeps back toward the origin at
\(s\approx-\wzero^2/(2\alpha)=-1/(RC)\). The slow pole becoming the dominant RC
lag is the limit you would expect physically: a huge series resistor makes the
capacitor charge through \(R\) alone.
\end{advancedbox}

\section{How Poles and Zeros Shape the Bode Plot}
Because the frequency response samples \(G(s)\) along the imaginary axis, the
pole-zero locations control the Bode plot directly: each pole bends the magnitude
down \(\SI{20}{\decibel}\) per decade and subtracts up to \(90^\circ\) of phase
near its corner; each zero bends it up \(\SI{20}{\decibel}\) per decade and adds
up to \(90^\circ\). A pole pair near the axis makes the resonant peak (height
\(\sim Q\), width \(2\alpha\)); a zero near the axis makes a notch. The next
chapter reads these plots in detail.

\section{Zeros in Radio Circuits}
Zeros are how circuits \emph{reject} energy. A series LC branch to ground has zero
impedance at its resonance, shorting that frequency to ground and placing a
transmission zero on the \(\jj\omega\) axis at \(\wzero=1/\sqrt{LC}\)---a trap. A
parallel LC branch in series with the signal path is the dual: it becomes an open
circuit at resonance, again blocking one frequency. Wherever the exam describes a
trap, a wave trap, or a notch rejecting a specific frequency, a zero has been
parked at that frequency.

\section{Worked Example: Poles of a Tuned Circuit}
Take a tuned LC circuit with \(L=\SI{10}{\micro\henry}\),
\(C=\SI{220}{\pico\farad}\), and series loss \(R=\SI{5}{\ohm}\). Then
\[
\wzero=\frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}\approx\SI{2.13e7}{\radian\per\second}
\ (f_0\approx\SI{3.39}{\mega\hertz}),
\qquad
\alpha=\frac{R}{2L}=\SI{2.5e5}{\radian\per\second},
\]
so \(\zeta=\alpha/\wzero\approx0.0117\) and \(Q=1/(2\zeta)\approx42.7\). The poles
sit at
\[
s=-\alpha\pm\jj\,\omega_d\approx-2.5\times10^{5}\pm\jj\,2.13\times10^{7}
\ \si{\radian\per\second}.
\]
The real part is about \(1/85\) of the imaginary part, so the poles are pressed
against the \(\jj\omega\) axis: a very high-\(Q\), sharply peaked circuit that
rings for roughly \(Q\approx43\) cycles. \Cref{ch:rlc} works this same circuit as
a resonator; here it is the same fact read as a pole location.

\begin{exambox}
The exam does not ask you to plot poles and zeros. It asks about resonant
frequency, \(Q\), bandwidth, selectivity, ringing, oscillator stability, filter
sharpness, and traps---all of which answer one question: \emph{where are the poles
and zeros?} Poles near the \(\jj\omega\) axis mean sharp and selective; poles on
the axis mean sustained oscillation; zeros on the axis mean a rejected frequency.
\end{exambox}
