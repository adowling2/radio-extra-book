\chapter{Frequency Response, Bode, and the s-Plane}
\label{ch:splane}
\label{ch:bode}
\Cref{ch:linsys} built first- and second-order systems in the time and
state-space view. This chapter is their frequency and complex-plane companion:
how the same systems respond to steady sinusoids (the Bode plot), and where their
behavior lives in the complex plane (poles, zeros, and the root locus). These are
the two classical design tools---Bode and root locus---and both are just the
transfer function \(G(s)\) read in a particular way.

\section{What a Bode Plot Shows}
A Bode plot is a pair of graphs against logarithmic frequency,
\[
20\log_{10}|G(\jj\omega)|
\quad\text{(magnitude, in dB)},
\qquad
\angle G(\jj\omega)
\quad\text{(phase)}.
\]
The magnitude curve shows gain or attenuation; the phase curve shows lead or lag.
Frequency is logarithmic, so equal ratios take equal space: a \emph{decade} is a
\(\times 10\) ratio and an \emph{octave} is a \(\times 2\) ratio, and
\(\SI{20}{\decibel}\)/decade \(\approx\SI{6}{\decibel}\)/octave. The frequency
response is the transfer function of \cref{ch:linsys} evaluated on the imaginary
axis, \(s=\jj\omega\).

\section{First-Order Response}
The first-order low-pass \(G(s)=1/(1+\tau s)\) has
\[
|G|=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+(\omega\tau)^2}},
\qquad
\angle G=-\tan^{-1}(\omega\tau).
\]
The magnitude is flat until the \emph{corner frequency} \(\omega_c=1/\tau\), then
rolls off at \(-\SI{20}{\decibel}\) per decade; the phase slides from \(0^\circ\)
to \(-90^\circ\), passing \(-45^\circ\) at the corner (\cref{fig:bode1}). The
corner is the same \(1/\tau\) that set the time constant in \cref{ch:linsys}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{bode_first_order_lowpass}
\caption{First-order low-pass Bode plot. The corner is at \(\omega_c=1/\tau\),
where the gain is \(-\SI{3}{\decibel}\) and the phase is \(-45^\circ\).}
\label{fig:bode1}
\end{figure}

\section{Building the Sketch from Poles and Zeros}
A Bode magnitude plot can be sketched by hand from the pole and zero locations,
because each contributes a straight-line asymptote that adds to the others:
\begin{itemize}
\item Start at the low-frequency gain (the DC value \(|G(0)|\)).
\item At each \textbf{pole} corner, bend the slope \emph{down} by
\(\SI{20}{\decibel}\) per decade and subtract up to \(90^\circ\) of phase.
\item At each \textbf{zero} corner, bend the slope \emph{up} by
\(\SI{20}{\decibel}\) per decade and add up to \(90^\circ\) of phase.
\item A corner sits at the frequency equal to that pole's or zero's distance from
the origin; an \(n\)-pole low-pass therefore rolls off at about
\(-20n\,\si{\decibel}\) per decade far above cutoff.
\end{itemize}
The exact curve rounds the corners---down \(\SI{3}{\decibel}\) at a first-order
pole, and peaking up at a lightly damped second-order pair (next section)---but
the asymptotes get the shape right.

\section{Second-Order Response: the Resonant Peak}
Sampling \(G(\jj\omega)\) near \(\omega_0\) brings the evaluation point close to
the pole pair, producing a resonant peak whose height grows with \(Q\)
(\cref{fig:bode2}). The flattest response with no peak occurs at
\(Q=1/\sqrt{2}\approx0.707\) (the Butterworth value); above it the magnitude
peaks near \(\omega_0\) by a factor of about \(Q\), and the peak sharpens as the
poles approach the \(\jj\omega\) axis.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{bode_second_order}
\caption{Second-order magnitude response. Higher \(Q\) (lower \(\zeta\)) means a
taller, sharper resonant peak, because the poles sit closer to the \(\jj\omega\)
axis.}
\label{fig:bode2}
\end{figure}

\section{Reading a Circuit off the Plot}
Given a measured or computed Bode plot, four features tell you what the circuit
does:
\begin{description}
\item[Passband and cutoff] the flat region is the passband; the
\(-\SI{3}{\decibel}\) frequency (magnitude \(1/\sqrt2\)) is the cutoff or corner.
\item[Roll-off slope] the far-out slope gives the order: \(-20n\,\si{\decibel}\)
per decade means \(n\) poles. Steeper skirts reject out-of-band signals harder.
\item[Resonant peak] a bump near \(\omega_0\) signals a high-\(Q\) pole pair; its
height grows with \(Q\) and its \(-\SI{3}{\decibel}\) width is the bandwidth
\(BW\approx f_0/Q\).
\item[Phase] the phase slope near a corner flags how much lead or lag the stage
adds---the quantity that governs stability once the stage sits inside a feedback
loop (\cref{ch:linsys}).
\end{description}

\section{The s-Plane: Poles, Zeros, and Stability}
The Bode plot samples \(G(s)\) on the imaginary axis; the s-plane shows the whole
picture---where \(G\) blows up (poles) and vanishes (zeros) across the complex
plane \(s=\sigma+\jj\omega\).

\begin{controlsbox}
\textbf{Refresher: poles and zeros.} Write \(G(s)=N(s)/D(s)\). The \emph{poles}
are the roots of \(D\) (the natural modes, equal to the eigenvalues of \(A\)); the
\emph{zeros} are the roots of \(N\) (frequencies transmitted as zero). An unforced
mode evolves as \(\ee^{s_i t}\) with \(s_i=\sigma_i+\jj\omega_i\), so the sign of
the real part is the entire stability story: left half-plane (\(\sigma_i<0\))
decays and is stable; the \(\jj\omega\) axis sustains a pure oscillation (the
knife-edge an oscillator sits on); the right half-plane grows and is unstable.
Zeros do not affect stability, but they shape how the stable modes appear.
\end{controlsbox}

\subsection{Reading the Pole Geometry}
For the second-order pole pair \(s=-\zeta\omega_0\pm\jj\omega_d\), two facts make
the plot readable by eye (\cref{fig:poles2}). First, while underdamped the poles
lie on a circle of radius \(\omega_0\), because
\(|s|^2=(\zeta\omega_0)^2+(\omega_0\sqrt{1-\zeta^2})^2=\omega_0^2\); the distance
from the origin is the natural frequency. Second, the angle \(\theta\) from the
negative real axis satisfies \(\cos\theta=\zeta\), so the angle encodes the
damping and therefore \(Q\). The real part \(\alpha=\zeta\omega_0\) is the
envelope decay rate \emph{and} half the half-power bandwidth: the full bandwidth is
\[
BW=2\alpha=\frac{\omega_0}{Q}\quad[\si{\radian\per\second}].
\]
\emph{Distance from the imaginary axis is half the bandwidth.} A pole hugging the
axis rings for a long time and shows a narrow, tall peak; a pole far from the axis
settles quickly and shows a broad, low peak.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.56\textwidth]{poles_second_order}
\caption{Second-order poles for several damping ratios. They ride a circle of
radius \(\omega_0\); the angle from the negative real axis has
\(\cos\theta=\zeta\). \(\zeta=0\) sits on the \(\jj\omega\) axis (undamped).}
\label{fig:poles2}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Zeros and the Pole-Zero Plot}
A pole-zero plot marks poles with \(\times\) and zeros with \(\circ\)
(\cref{fig:pz-notch}). Poles set the resonance and damping; a zero on or near the
\(\jj\omega\) axis pulls the magnitude \emph{down} toward a null at that
frequency---the mechanism of a notch, trap, or band-stop.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.52\textwidth]{poles_zeros_notch}
\caption{Pole-zero plot of a notch response. The complex pole pair
(\(\times\)) just inside the left half-plane sets the resonance; the transmission
zeros (\(\circ\)) on the \(\jj\omega\) axis force the output to zero at the
rejection frequency \(\omega_0\).}
\label{fig:pz-notch}
\end{figure}

\section{Root Locus}
When a parameter varies---a resistance, a gain, a feedback fraction---the poles
move, tracing a \emph{root locus}. For the canonical second-order system
\(s^2+2\zeta\omega_0 s+\omega_0^2\), sweeping the damping \(\zeta\) from zero
upward traces the path in \cref{fig:rootlocus2}: the poles begin on the
\(\jj\omega\) axis at \(\pm\jj\omega_0\) (undamped), arc along the circle of
radius \(\omega_0\) into the left half-plane, meet at \(-\omega_0\) at critical
damping (\(\zeta=1\)), then split and travel apart along the negative real axis
(overdamped).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.58\textwidth]{rootlocus_second_order}
\caption{Root locus of a second-order system as damping increases. Poles begin
on the \(\jj\omega\) axis (undamped), ride the \(\omega_0\) circle to \(-\omega_0\)
at critical damping, then separate along the real axis (overdamped).}
\label{fig:rootlocus2}
\end{figure}

The series RLC circuit of \cref{ch:rlc} is exactly this locus with
\(\zeta=\tfrac{R}{2}\sqrt{C/L}\), so sweeping \(R\) walks the poles from a pure
LC oscillation (\(R=0\)) to a heavily damped pair (\cref{fig:rlc-rootlocus}).

\section{Zeros in Radio Circuits}
Zeros are how circuits \emph{reject} energy. A series LC branch to ground has zero
impedance at its resonance, shorting that frequency to ground and placing a
transmission zero on the \(\jj\omega\) axis at \(\wzero=1/\sqrt{LC}\)---a trap. A
parallel LC branch in series with the signal path is the dual: it becomes an open
circuit at resonance, again blocking one frequency. Wherever the exam describes a
trap, a wave trap, or a notch rejecting a specific frequency, a zero has been
parked at that frequency.

\section{Worked Example: Poles of a Tuned Circuit}
Take a tuned LC circuit with \(L=\SI{10}{\micro\henry}\),
\(C=\SI{220}{\pico\farad}\), and series loss \(R=\SI{5}{\ohm}\). Then
\[
\wzero=\frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}\approx\SI{2.13e7}{\radian\per\second}
\ (f_0\approx\SI{3.39}{\mega\hertz}),
\qquad
\alpha=\frac{R}{2L}=\SI{2.5e5}{\radian\per\second},
\]
so \(\zeta=\alpha/\wzero\approx0.0117\) and \(Q=1/(2\zeta)\approx42.6\). The poles
sit at \(s=-\alpha\pm\jj\,\omega_d\approx-2.5\times10^{5}\pm\jj\,2.13\times10^{7}\
\si{\radian\per\second}\): the real part is about \(1/85\) of the imaginary part,
so the poles are pressed against the \(\jj\omega\) axis---a very high-\(Q\),
sharply peaked circuit that rings for roughly \(Q\approx43\) cycles.
\Cref{ch:rlc} works this same circuit as a resonator.

\begin{exambox}
The exam does not ask you to plot poles or draw Bode diagrams. It asks about
cutoff, roll-off slope, resonant frequency, \(Q\), bandwidth, selectivity,
ringing, oscillator stability, filter sharpness, and traps---all of which answer
one question: \emph{where are the poles and zeros?} Near the \(\jj\omega\) axis
means sharp and selective; on the axis means sustained oscillation; a zero on the
axis means a rejected frequency.
\end{exambox}
