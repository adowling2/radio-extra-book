\chapter{RC Circuits}
\label{ch:rc}
RC circuits appear as coupling and bypass networks, timing circuits, audio
filters, and detector components. An RC circuit is the electrical embodiment of
the \emph{first-order system} of \cref{ch:linsys}: one energy store, one time
constant, one real pole. This chapter walks the Part~III recipe end to end.

\section{The Circuit}
Take a source \(u(t)\) driving a resistor \(R\) in series with a capacitor \(C\),
with the output taken across the capacitor (\cref{fig:rc-schematic}). The natural
state variable is the capacitor voltage \(v_C\), because it holds the stored
energy.

\centering
\begin{circuitikz}[scale=1]

\end{circuitikz}
\caption{Series RC circuit with the output taken across the capacitor (a

\label{fig:rc-schematic}
\end{figure}

\section{From Schematic to Differential Equation}
KVL around the loop says the source equals the resistor drop plus the capacitor
voltage, and the capacitor law relates its current to its rate of voltage change:
\[
u(t)=R\,i(t)+v_C(t),
\qquad
i(t)=C\frac{\dd v_C}{\dd t}.
\]
Substituting the second into the first,
\[
\boxed{\,RC\frac{\dd v_C}{\dd t}+v_C=u(t).\,}
\]

\section{State-Space Form}
With the single state \(x=v_C\),
\[
\dot{x}=-\frac{1}{RC}\,x+\frac{1}{RC}\,u,
\qquad
y=x=v_C .
\]
There is one state because there is one independent energy store, so there is one
pole, at \(s=-1/(RC)\).

\section{Matching the Generic First-Order System}
Compare with the canonical first-order system \(\tau\dot y+y=Ku\) from
\cref{ch:linsys}. They are identical with
\[
\tau=RC,
\qquad
K=1,
\qquad
s_{\text{pole}}=-\frac{1}{\tau}=-\frac{1}{RC}.
\]
Every first-order result from \cref{ch:linsys} now applies with no extra work:
the step response, the \(63.2\%\) rule, the corner frequency, and the Bode shape.

\section{Transient Response}
For a capacitor charging from a DC step of height \(E\), and for discharge from
initial voltage \(V_0\),
\[
v_C(t)=E\left(1-\ee^{-t/RC}\right),
\qquad
v_C(t)=V_0\,\ee^{-t/RC}.
\]
This is the first-order response of \cref{ch:linsys} read as a capacitor voltage
(\cref{fig:rc-transient}): after one time constant \(\tau=RC\) the capacitor has
charged to \(63.2\%\) of \(E\) (or discharged to \(36.8\%\)), and after about
\(5\tau\) it is settled.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{rc_transient}
\caption{RC capacitor-voltage transient. Charging and discharging are mirror
exponentials with the same time constant \(\tau=RC\); one \(\tau\) covers
\(63.2\%\) of the change.}
\label{fig:rc-transient}
\end{figure}

\section{Frequency Response: Low-Pass and High-Pass}
The same circuit is a low-pass or a high-pass filter depending on where the
output is taken:
\[
G_C(\jj\omega)=\frac{1}{1+\jj\omega RC}\ \text{(across }C\text{, low-pass)},
\qquad
G_R(\jj\omega)=\frac{\jj\omega RC}{1+\jj\omega RC}\ \text{(across }R\text{, high-pass)}.
\]
Both share the corner frequency \(\omega_c=1/(RC)\), where the gain is
\(-\SI{3}{\decibel}\). The low-pass passes slow signals and rolls off high ones
(\cref{fig:bode1}); the high-pass does the reverse (\cref{fig:rc-highpass}).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{bode_first_order_highpass}
\caption{RC high-pass response (output across \(R\)): \(-\SI{20}{\decibel}\) per
decade rise below the corner, flat above, phase from \(+90^\circ\) to
\(0^\circ\).}
\label{fig:rc-highpass}
\end{figure}

\section{The Pole and the Root Locus}
The single pole sits on the negative real axis at \(-1/(RC)\). Changing \(R\) or
\(C\) slides it along that axis: a larger \(RC\) (slower circuit, lower cutoff)
moves the pole toward the origin; a smaller \(RC\) moves it left toward faster
response (\cref{fig:rc-rootlocus}). Because the pole never leaves the real axis,
an RC circuit cannot overshoot or ring---the first-order signature.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{rootlocus_first_order}
\caption{Root locus of the RC pole. The single real pole \(-1/(RC)\) slides along
the negative real axis as the time constant changes; it never becomes complex, so
there is no ringing.}
\label{fig:rc-rootlocus}
\end{figure}

\begin{controlsbox}
\textbf{Connection.} The RC circuit is the textbook first-order lag. Its cutoff
frequency, its \(-\SI{20}{\decibel}\)/decade roll-off, and its \(63.2\%\)-per-time-constant
step are three faces of the single pole at \(-1/\tau\). Cascading RC stages adds
poles and steepens the roll-off, at the cost of accumulated phase lag---the same
bookkeeping as adding first-order lags in a control loop.
\end{controlsbox}

\section{Energy Interpretation}
The capacitor stores \(E_C=\tfrac12 Cv_C^2\). During charging, source energy
splits between the electric field and resistive heat; during discharge, the
stored field energy is dissipated in \(R\). The time constant \(\tau=RC\) is how
long that energy exchange takes.

\begin{unitsbox}
\[
RC=\si{\second},\qquad
\omega_c=\frac{1}{RC}=\si{\per\second},\qquad
f_c=\frac{1}{2\pi RC}=\si{\hertz}.
\]
\end{unitsbox}

\begin{mistakebox}
A capacitor is an open circuit only at DC steady state. During a transient or at
AC, its current \(i=C\,\dd v_C/\dd t\) is generally nonzero.
\end{mistakebox}

\section{Worked Example}
Let \(R=\SI{4.7}{\kilo\ohm}\) and \(C=\SI{100}{\nano\farad}\). Then
\[
\tau=RC=(4.7\times10^3)(100\times10^{-9})=\SI{4.70e-4}{\second},
\qquad
f_c=\frac{1}{2\pi\tau}\approx\SI{339}{\hertz}.
\]
The pole is at \(-1/\tau\approx-\SI{2130}{\per\second}\). Reading the low-pass
Bode plot (\cref{fig:bode1}) at this corner: signals well below \SI{339}{\hertz}
pass nearly unattenuated, signals a decade above (\(\sim\!\SI{3.4}{\kilo\hertz}\))
are down about \SI{20}{\decibel}, and the phase lag is \(45^\circ\) right at the
corner. In the time domain the same circuit settles a step in roughly
\(5\tau\approx\SI{2.4}{\milli\second}\).
